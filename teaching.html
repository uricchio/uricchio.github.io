Teaching & mentoring

Teaching philosophy

As an IRACDA postdoc, I get to spend a substantial portion of my effort training in evidence-based teaching and instructing at SJSU. In my capacity as an instructor, I strive to apply scientific teaching principles in the context of a student-centered learning environment.

Postdoc pedagogy minisymposia

Postdocs tend to spend the vast majority of their time performing research, and probably rightly so. But if and when we are successful in making the transition to academic faculty, a substantial portion of our job responsibilities will be to teach and train the next generation of scientists. To help address this gap in postdoc training, my collaborator Whitney Heavner and I designed and implemented a series of teaching minisymposia for Stanford postdocs. We obtained competitive funding from the Stanford Teaching and Mentoring Academy to recruit 4 experts in pedagogical techniques to Stanford's campus to train postdocs. We focused on four core areas of evidence-based STEM teaching, including active learning, diversity in the classroom, quantitative reasoning, and methods of assessment. Whitney created a nice webpage about our series of events, which you can find here.

Stanford biocore explorations for undergraduates

I designed and taught a biocore exploration along with Alison Feder at Stanford in Fall 2015. Biocore explorations are an opportunity for postdoctoral and graduate student researchers to interact with undergraduates enrolled in the core biology course. Instructors typically design an interactive workshop based on their research area. Alison and I designed a course that combined some quantitative reasoning with an exploration of human genetic data. Our central question was "is there evidence in human genetic data that human traits are under selection?"

We modeled our workshop on a recent study that used genetic data to provide evidence that natural selection has shaped human height. The students used computer code that we designed to explore the impact of selection on quantitative traits by changing model paremeters, and then used real human genomic data to test their intuition.

Our goal in this course was to develop a computationally-minded set of activities that the students could interact with, in the spirit of active-learning, which I think we (mostly!) achieved. In future versions of this course, I would love to spend more time developing computational skills so the students can write their own code, and replicate the analyses in multiple academic papers. Stanford CEHG high school outreach

The Center for Computational, Evolutionary, and Human Genomics recently started a series of outreach events. Our goal is to inspire high school students of diverse backgrounds to consider pursuing careers in science, and especially genetics. In 2015, I helped to implement the inaugural event, in which we invited students from Sequoia High School to visit Stanford labs and speak with a broad range of graduate students and postdocs. One of my main contributions was organizing and designing a pamphlet for high schoolers about studying science in college, which you can find here.
