Welcome

Welcome to my research page. I am a postdoctoral fellow of the Center for Comptational, Evolutionary and Human Genomics in Noah Rosenberg's lab in the Department of Biology at Stanford University, where I study the impact of evolutionary forces on patterns of DNA sequence variation in human populations. I develop computational tools that are (I hope!) useful for other researchers interested in parametric models in evolutionary genetics. I also have a strong interest in teaching, mentoring, science education policy, and curricular design.

Prior to arriving at Stanford, I was a graduate student with Ryan Hernandez at UCSF, a scientific programmer with Dan Nicolae and Carole Ober at the University of Chicago, a research technician at UCLA with Lloyd Miller (now at Johns Hopkins University), and an undergraduate physics major studying with Arjendu Pattanayak at Carleton College.

Research Interests

Understanding how selection impacts genetic architecture and tests of association

Natural selection acts to promote advantageous genetic variation and to constrain deleterious genetic variation. For example, it is now well established that mutations conferring the ability to metabolize lactose in adulthood were advantageous in the proto-European population thousands of years ago. This makes very good sense as an evolutionary story: adults who could metabolize lactose were able to benefit from the domestication of cows, since they could use milk as a highly reliable food source. Lactose intolerant individuals would not have shared this advantage.

In contrast, it is reasonable to think that some genetic variants might be disadvantageous. For example, let's consider a human disease that has a genetic risk component. If this disease decreases the chance of producing fertile offspring (say, by reducing the expected lifespan of an affected individual), then it is reasonable to suppose that natural selection might keep alleles that increase disease risk at low frequency in the population.

But this now introduces an interesting quandary. If natural selection acts to constrain genetic variation that contributes to disease, then why are there so many common, heritable human diseases, such as asthma, cardiovascular disease, and diabetes? My work (and the work of many others in the field of human population genetics), has sought to address this question in a quantitative framework using population genetics theory. There are many possible explanations, including

  • Natural selection does not actually act on disease variation for common human diseases.
  • Heritable human diseases are not as heritable as we think they are (i.e., genetics plays a smaller role than environment).
  • Disease alleles are detrimental in the context of the disease, but advantageous for another reason (e.g., a disease variant increased the rate of surviving childhood in ancestral human populations, but shortens the expected lifespan in adulthood). As a result, disease variants can rise to higher frequency because these effects (partially) cancel. This is an example of pleiotropy.
  • Disease variants are actually under very strong negative selection, and many different sites in the 3 billion base pairs of the human genome confer disease risk.
To date, it's been very difficult to tease apart these possible explanations and make sense of how selection does (or does not) act on common, heritable human diseases. My work has mostly focused on the last two possibilities. I have used theoretical population genetic models of natural selection and complex phenotypes to model recent (~100,000 yrs) human evolutionary history. Together with my PhD advisor Ryan Hernandez and our collaborator John Witte, I showed that state-of-the-art techniques for detecting risk loci in the genome have lower power than one would hope when selection acts most strongly on phenotypes. Our work doesn't say anything about whether we expect selection to act on phenotypes, just that it's difficult to find the causal genetic loci when selection is strong. As a result, it is difficult to quantitatively compare all the possible explanations above, because we lack the tools to definitively rule out a role for rare alleles under selection in human phenotypes. However, I am very hopeful that the current generation of tools can be modified and extended to improve their performance, at least for some disease models.

How does linked selection impact genetic variation?

The human genome consists of 23 chromosomes, each inherited in a single copy from each parent. As a result, genetic sites that are on a single chromosome are "linked", meaning that they tend to be passed on to offspring together. This immediately raises an important point about natural selection: if selection acts on a given genetic variant, then alleles that are nearby on the same chromosome will also be subject to the same selective force. Over long periods of time, recombination (a genetic mechanism for patching paternal and maternal chromosomes together in gametes), will tend to swap alleles onto different chromosomes, ameliorating the effect of linked selection.

Traditionally, most of our best theoretical tools for investigating selection have focused on modeling selection on individual sites (and ignoring linkage), or modeling simple linkage schemes in which natural selection follows very simple patterns. Some of my ongoing work seeks to extend these simple models by incorporating more complex models through the use of computer simulations. I am generally interested in how linked selection (both of advantageous alleles and deleterious alleles) may have impacted the human genome and the genomes of other organisms. This work relies heavily on the ability to perform computationally efficient simulations of linked selection.

Jointly inferring selection and demography

When natural selection acts to constrain deleterious genetic variation, it tends to cause an up-tick in the fraction of alleles in a population that are very-rare. As a result, if we were to sequence the genomes of a large number of humans, we could test whether natural selection has acted on our genomes simply by counting the number of individuals that carry each genetic variant in our dataset. If we observe an excess of rare alleles relative to a very simple theoretical calculation, we can infer that selection has acted to shape patterns of diversity in humans.

Unfortunately, this very simple approach is not correct. Other (non-selective) evolutionary events can affect the proportion of sites in the genome that are present, especially changes in population size. In particular, when populations grow rapidly, they tend to accumulate more rare alleles. Hence, it is challenging to disentangle how selection and demography have both acted to affect human genetic variation.

Some of my ongoing work seeks to attack this problem by building inference tools using simulations and Approximate Bayesian Computation, as well as extending the highly influential Poisson Random Field model.
